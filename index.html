01 / Food & Restaurants

Dining is joy — but table time feels different

🇪🇺 In Europe...

In many European cities, meals can be long, relaxed, and social. Terraces, cafés, wine, conversation, and staying after the meal are part of the experience.

Slow dining Café culture Conversation Terraces

🇯🇵 In Japan / Osaka...

Dining is also important, but busy restaurants often value smooth turnover. At ramen shops, sushi counters, small cafés, and popular local restaurants, guests are expected to enjoy the meal without staying too long when others are waiting.

No tipping Quieter voice Respect waiting guests Say “Sumimasen”
✅ Good manners in Osaka
  • Use a softer voice in small restaurants.
  • Say “Sumimasen” to call staff politely.
  • Leave your seat after eating if the shop is crowded.
  • Say “Arigatou” or “Gochisousama” instead of tipping.
⚠️ Be careful
  • Do not leave tips on the table. Staff may think you forgot money.
  • A long café-style stay is better at spacious cafés or hotel lounges.
  • Do not handle chopsticks, dishes, or shared sauces carelessly.

02 / Public Spaces

Public spaces are lively in Europe, quieter in Japan

🇪🇺 In Europe...

Squares, parks, streets, cafés, and markets are places to meet, talk, take photos, and enjoy the city openly. Public space often feels like part of social life.

Plazas Open talk Street culture Photos

🇯🇵 In Japan / Osaka...

Stations, shopping streets, department stores, and tourist spots are shared spaces. People usually try not to block movement, make loud noise, or disturb others who are passing by.

Shared space Keep moving Carry trash Photo awareness
✅ Good manners in Osaka
  • Step to the side before checking your phone or map.
  • Use a moderate voice in stations and shopping streets.
  • Carry a small trash bag because bins can be hard to find.
  • Check the background before taking photos.
⚠️ Be careful
  • Do not stop in the middle of a walkway.
  • Do not film people closely without permission.
  • Do not eat while walking in crowded shopping streets unless the area allows it.
🚶
03 / Lines & Personal Space

In Japan, the line is almost a silent promise

🇪🇺 In Europe...

Queue culture differs by country. In some places, people ask directly, move flexibly, or communicate clearly to find the right order. Direct communication can be natural.

Country differences Direct questions Flexible flow

🇯🇵 In Japan / Osaka...

In Japan, the order of the line is very important. At stations, convenience stores, bus stops, ticket counters, and popular restaurants, people naturally form a neat line.

Find the end No cutting Wait quietly Follow floor signs
✅ Good manners in Osaka
  • Look for “最後尾” signs or the end of the queue.
  • Leave a little space in front of you.
  • Wait calmly even if the line moves slowly.
  • Follow arrows and floor markings at stations.
⚠️ Be careful
  • Do not cut in, even if the line looks unclear.
  • Do not push forward when boarding trains or elevators.
  • A loud complaint may feel uncomfortable in quiet spaces.
🚆
04 / Public Transport

Japanese trains are quiet shared rooms on wheels

🇪🇺 In Europe...

Talking on trains, moving with large luggage, and relaxed travel behavior can be common, especially on longer journeys. Train culture varies widely across Europe.

Conversation Luggage travel Long journeys Relaxed mood

🇯🇵 In Japan / Osaka...

Trains and subways are quiet shared spaces. Many people commute, sleep, read, study, or work, so passengers help keep the environment calm.

No phone calls Let people exit first Bag in front No sound leakage
✅ Good manners in Osaka
  • Speak quietly if you need to talk.
  • Do not make phone calls on the train.
  • Let passengers get off before you board.
  • Place suitcases by your feet or near the side.
  • Wear headphones and keep volume low.
⚠️ Be careful
  • Large luggage can block doors and aisles.
  • Backpacks can hit people in crowded cars.
  • Priority seats should be offered to those who need them.
⛩️
05 / Temples & Heritage

Enjoy the beauty — and respect the quiet spirit

🇪🇺 In Europe...

Churches, cathedrals, castles, museums, old towns, and historic architecture are often enjoyed as cultural heritage and sightseeing spots. Photography and architectural appreciation are natural parts of travel.

Cathedrals Museums Old towns Architecture

🇯🇵 In Japan / Osaka...

Shrines and temples are beautiful tourist sites, but they are also active places of prayer. Local people still visit them for festivals, weddings, New Year prayers, and everyday wishes.

Prayer space Quiet respect No-touch heritage Follow signs
✅ Good manners in Osaka
  • Walk calmly around shrine and temple areas.
  • Follow “No Photography” signs.
  • Do not enter restricted areas.
  • Do not touch cultural properties, gates, statues, or old wooden buildings.
  • Enjoy the silence as part of the culture.
⚠️ Be careful
  • Do not use sacred spaces as photo stages if people are praying nearby.
  • Do not climb, lean on, or sit on heritage structures.
  • Tripods and flash may be restricted in many places.

A ramen shop is crowded. You finished eating. What should you do?

Stay for a long chat, or give the seat to the next guest?

Answer

Give the seat to the next guest. In Japan, this shows respect for the restaurant and other customers.

🚆

Your phone rings on the train. What is best?

Answer normally, or silence it and call later?

Answer

Silence it and call later. Japanese trains are treated as quiet shared spaces.

⛩️

You see a beautiful shrine gate. Can you touch or climb it for a photo?

Looks cool, but is it okay?

Answer

No. Shrines and heritage structures should be treated with respect. Enjoy the photo without touching the structure.
